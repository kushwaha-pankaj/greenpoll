\subsection{Transfer learning for object detection}

Modern detectors are almost always trained on top of a pretrained backbone. The general argument for this practice was made early --- features in the lower layers of a deep network are largely task-agnostic and re-usable~\cite{yosinski2014transferable} --- and the empirical case has only gotten stronger as datasets and models have scaled~\cite{sun2017revisiting, zhuang2021tlsurvey}. For object detection specifically, COCO-pretrained backbones~\cite{lin2014coco, lin2017fpn, he2016resnet} are the de facto standard starting point in modern frameworks~\cite{terven2023yolo, jocher2023ultralytics}. Most of this literature assumes a moderate-to-large target dataset; the regime we study here, where the target is a single crop with ten to two hundred labels, has received less systematic attention.

A small but growing body of work has examined the choice of \emph{which} layers to update when fine-tuning. The empirical analysis closest to our work is~\cite{garg2025fine}, which studies progressive backbone unfreezing on YOLOv8 for fruit detection and finds that freezing modules 0--9 (the YOLOv8n backbone in the Ultralytics convention) yields the best target accuracy on a six-class fruit benchmark while preserving COCO performance. Their result motivated our \texttt{freeze=10} choice. The Ultralytics documentation itself recommends the same setting for "small datasets" and "similar feature domains between datasets," both of which describe our setup.

\subsection{Few-shot object detection}

The few-shot object detection (FSOD) literature studies the same problem we care about --- training a detector with very few labels per class --- but typically does so under a meta-learning setup where many auxiliary classes are available at training time and the test classes appear with $K$ examples each~\cite{kang2019fsod, wang2020fsdetview}. \cite{kohler2023fsdetsurvey} surveys the area and notes that the strongest practical methods are often the simplest: pretrain on a base set, then fine-tune the head on the few-shot classes with the backbone partly or fully frozen. This is structurally what we do, with the substitution that our "base set" is a domain-matched joint flower corpus rather than a generic image dataset.

A few authors have looked specifically at cross-domain few-shot detection, where the target distribution shifts away from the pretraining distribution. The general finding is that domain matching helps when labels are scarce and matters less when labels are plentiful~\cite{wang2020fsdetview, kohler2023fsdetsurvey}. Our budget-dependent result is consistent with that pattern, but our setting differs in that both source and target are agricultural and the "shift" is between crops rather than between domains in the more dramatic sense of moving from natural images to medical scans.

\subsection{Agricultural computer vision}

Deep learning for agriculture is a substantial subfield in its own right~\cite{kamilaris2018review}. Most work targets a single crop or a small set of related crops, often with bespoke datasets collected by the authors. Apple-flower detection in orchards~\cite{dias2018apple, tian2019apple} and fruit detection across several crop types~\cite{genemola2020fruit} are representative examples. The cross-crop transfer question --- given a detector trained on apple, strawberry, and tomato flowers, how well does it adapt to a new crop such as kiwi --- has been less studied, in part because it requires aggregating multiple per-crop datasets with different annotation conventions. Our harmonization step addresses that.

\subsection{Robotic pollination}

Robotic pollination systems have appeared in the literature for both open-field and indoor crops~\cite{strader2019pollination}. The perception requirements for these systems are stricter than for fruit detection because pollination is time-sensitive: the robot must locate every open flower before its receptive window closes. Few-shot adaptation to a new crop is therefore not a hypothetical problem --- a working pollination system that ships to a new greenhouse will need to detect a crop the manufacturer never trained on, and it will need to do so with whatever labels the operator can provide in an afternoon.

\subsection{Position}

Our contribution sits at the intersection of these four threads. We do not propose a new detector architecture, a new meta-learning algorithm, or a new domain-adaptation loss. We provide an empirical answer to a deployment-relevant question: given a small joint multi-crop dataset, what fine-tuning protocol gets the most out of the few labels you have for a new crop? The answer, on the kiwi target with apple, strawberry, and tomato as source, is to pretrain jointly and then fine-tune with the backbone frozen.
