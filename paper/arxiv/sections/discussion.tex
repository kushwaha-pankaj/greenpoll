\subsection{Why does freezing rescue the small-budget collapse?}

The unfrozen CrossPoll runs at $n{=}25$ exhibit a textbook small-batch instability. With 25 training images and a per-step batch of 16, each epoch contains one full mini-batch and one partial mini-batch of nine images. The partial batch contributes a noisy gradient estimate, and three consecutive noisy updates are enough to push a near-converged head into a region of weight space from which it does not recover within the patience window. Freezing the backbone removes more than 60\% of trainable parameters from the gradient path, which both reduces the effective gradient noise and protects the joint-pretrain features that already encode "what a flower looks like across crops." This interpretation matches the empirical finding of~\cite{gandhi2025fine} on a related fruit-detection benchmark, where partial backbone unfreezing was the right choice for a domain-specialized starting point.

The same explanation accounts for the $n{=}10$ result. COCO transfer cannot reliably converge from 10 labels because the head must learn the "kiwi flower" concept from scratch on top of generic features, and one in three seeds fails to bridge that gap. A joint pretrain that already knows about apple, strawberry, and tomato flowers needs only to specialize the existing concept; with the backbone frozen, the few gradient updates the head receives are spent productively on that specialization rather than on first-principles concept learning.

\subsection{Why does COCO transfer regain the lead at $n \geq 50$?}

Once enough labels are available, the head can adapt without forcing destructive backbone updates, and full fine-tuning from a generic but high-capacity pretrain has a structural advantage. COCO covers a much larger and more diverse image distribution than our 11{,}300-image joint flower set, and the backbone's low-level feature bank is correspondingly broader. The harmonized joint pretrain trades that breadth for a domain match. At small budgets the trade is worth it; at moderate budgets the breadth advantage reasserts itself.

We did not tune fine-tuning hyperparameters separately per method. In particular, the initial learning rate of $10^{-4}$ was chosen to be appropriate for the joint pretrain checkpoint and was applied uniformly to scratch and COCO runs. A higher learning rate would likely improve the from-scratch baseline at the lowest budgets and might narrow the COCO advantage at moderate budgets. We flag this as a methodological limitation rather than try to retroactively reinterpret the existing numbers.

\subsection{Practical recommendation}

For a greenhouse-pollination deployment that needs to add a new crop with minimal annotation effort, the empirically supported recipe is: collect a joint multi-crop dataset of source flowers, harmonize labels to a single class, pretrain with COCO initialization, and fine-tune on the new crop with the backbone frozen. At ten labels this protocol reaches roughly 63\% test mAP@0.5 with low cross-seed variance. At twenty-five it matches the strongest baseline. This is the regime where the method is most useful: a beekeeper or grower can label a couple of dozen images by hand and obtain a working detector in well under an hour of fine-tune time on a free-tier GPU.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our evaluation has a single held-out crop (kiwi). The pattern we report --- joint pretraining helps at small budgets, COCO catches up at moderate budgets --- needs to be checked on at least one other target crop before we can claim it generalizes. The kiwi training split has only 181 images, so the upper end of the budget sweep is not really a "lots of data" regime; it is a "use everything you have" regime. We tested only the YOLOv8n architecture; the constants in our argument (gradient noise from small batches, backbone-vs-head capacity ratio) all depend on architecture, and the optimal freeze depth on a larger backbone may differ. We tested one freeze depth (\texttt{freeze=10}) and did not run an ablation across the natural choices of 5, 15, and 22. The fine-tune learning rate was not tuned per method, as noted above. Finally, our test set is small (61 images, 369 instances), which is appropriate for the few-shot framing but means the achievable test mAP@0.5{:}0.95 likely caps around 0.65 regardless of method.

None of these limitations are fatal. Each one is a clear next experiment, and several can be answered in a single follow-up Kaggle session using the same code and the same datasets.
