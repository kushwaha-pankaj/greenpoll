We set out to test whether a small joint multi-crop pretrain could outperform standard COCO transfer for few-shot flower detection on a new crop. The empirical answer is conditional. Naive full fine-tuning of the joint pretrain hurts: at $n{=}25$ labels it collapses to roughly a third of COCO's accuracy because the joint pretrain's domain-specialized features are fragile under small-batch gradient noise. Adding backbone freezing fixes the collapse and produces the strongest results we have at the smallest budgets, beating COCO transfer by 27 points at $n{=}10$ with substantially lower variance across seeds. From $n{=}50$ upward COCO transfer regains a small lead, because the breadth advantage of a generic pretrain reasserts itself once the head can update freely without destabilizing the backbone.

The next experiments are clear. We need to repeat the sweep with a second held-out crop to confirm that the small-budget freeze advantage generalizes beyond kiwi; we need to ablate the freeze depth (5, 10, 15, 22) to find the operating point on a per-architecture basis; and we need to retune the fine-tune learning rate per method so that the COCO and scratch baselines are presented at their best. With those follow-ups in place, the same machinery in this paper produces a more rigorous argument and we hope a more useful recipe for practitioners building greenhouse-pollination perception systems.
